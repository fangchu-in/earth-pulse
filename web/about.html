Observing the Earth,
over time

Earth Pulse is a ground-level environmental observatory on the edge of the Western Ghats — tracking air quality, hyperlocal weather, sound, birds, and plants from one location, continuously, since January 2022.

The objective

To build the most detailed, long-term, freely accessible environmental record of a single location in urban India — combining air quality, hyperlocal microclimate, acoustic biodiversity, plant diversity, and visual documentation into one continuous, open dataset.

Most environmental data is regional and aggregated. Earth Pulse is hyperlocal and hourly. The goal is to reveal patterns that regional data cannot — the morning AQI spike before traffic, the exact temperature when the first monsoon cloud arrives, the dawn chorus shifting as seasons change, the plants that anchor the hill's ecology.

Over time this becomes more than data. It becomes memory, evidence, and a foundation for understanding how a place changes — accessible not just to researchers, but to anyone curious about the world around them.

Why this place matters

The Western Ghats edge

Baner Hill sits at 560 metres on the eastern edge of the Western Ghats — a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of Earth's eight biodiversity hotspots. These mountains are older than the Himalayas, hold 30% of India's plant and animal species within less than 6% of its land area, and are monitored by almost no one at the hyperlocal level.

Baner is where the city meets the Sahyadri. On one side: Pune's fastest-growing suburb, with traffic, construction, and rising temperatures. On the other: a forested hill where bee-eaters nest, Indian rollers flash overhead, and the monsoon arrives two days before the city centre. Earth Pulse sits exactly at that edge — recording both worlds, continuously.

Earth Pulse is the first continuous, open, citizen-science environmental observatory on this edge — recording hourly air quality, hyperlocal weather, acoustic bird diversity, plant life, and hill photography since January 2022.

Why one place, over time

Depth over breadth

Most environmental monitoring moves fast and wide — snapshots from satellites, monthly averages, data that tells you a region is changing but cannot tell you when, or exactly how, or what it felt and sounded like when it did.

Earth Pulse moves slowly and deep. The same sensors, the same location, every hour, for years. That consistency is the point. It is what allows a question like "has the monsoon been arriving later since 2022?" or "does air quality drop when the hill birds go quiet?" to have a real answer, grounded in local data rather than extrapolated from somewhere else.

Sustained attention to one place is its own kind of science — and its own kind of care. Every reading logged is a small act of paying attention to something that most people pass on the way to somewhere else.

How it works

Methodology

Weather Union ZWL008983 Google Air Quality API Open-Meteo CAMS Copernicus BirdNET AI v2.4 · Two nodes PlantNet · Plant identification Trefle · Botanical data Gemini 2.5 Flash Lite · AI insights Sentinel-2 · NDVI satellite NDSI · Soundscape index

Hyperlocal weather

Since October 2025, temperature, humidity, wind, and rainfall are sourced from Weather Union station ZWL008983 — a physical AWS sensor 2 km from Baner Hill. Data is logged every 15 minutes. Before October 2025, Open-Meteo ECMWF model data provides the historical baseline.

📡

Why this matters: Regional weather models can be off by 3–4°C for microclimates at the Western Ghats edge. A physical sensor 2 km away is significantly more accurate for Baner's conditions.

Air quality

Hourly AQI from two independent sources. Copernicus CAMS via Open-Meteo provides the EU AQI (0–100) with PM2.5, PM10, NO₂, ozone, CO, SO₂, and ammonia — the same dataset used by European environmental agencies. Google Air Quality API provides India AQI (0–500) using India's national CPCB pollutant weighting. Both are stored side by side every hour since January 2022 and displayed separately — the two scales are never mixed or converted.

Acoustic bird detection — two nodes

BirdNET v2.4 by Cornell Lab of Ornithology runs continuously on two Raspberry Pi nodes. The east-facing node covers the Sahyadri foothills horizon; the south-facing node captures the Baner Hill soundscape directly. Each detection is logged with confidence score and matched to the climate reading at that exact hour — enabling correlations between air quality, weather, and bird presence that single-node monitoring cannot provide.

Plant identification

Photos submitted to identify.html are processed through PlantNet (a neural net trained on 50,000+ plant species) for identification, then cross-referenced with Trefle for botanical metadata — growth rate, drought tolerance, height — and Gemini 2.5 Flash Lite for a conservation profile specific to the Western Ghats. Submissions within 10 km of Baner Hill are automatically added to the hill_trees database.

Soundscape health (NDSI)

The Normalised Difference Soundscape Index measures biophony (birds, insects, rain) against anthrophony (traffic, construction, machinery) in the audio spectrum. A score near +1 indicates a healthy natural soundscape; near −1 indicates urban noise dominance. At Baner, scores range from −0.85 at midday peak traffic to +0.70 during the pre-dawn chorus — the first continuous acoustic health record for this location.

Vegetation monitoring (NDVI)

Baner Hill's canopy health is tracked using the Normalised Difference Vegetation Index from Landsat satellite imagery (1990–2022 annual) and Sentinel-2 (weekly, 2023+). NDVI above 0.45 indicates healthy canopy cover. The 1990 baseline enables long-term comparison — showing how the hill has changed across three decades of urbanisation on its edges.

AI insights (Gemini 2.5 Flash Lite)

Gemini 2.5 Flash Lite adds plain-English interpretation to climate and bird data across the site — explaining what the numbers mean, flagging unusual readings, and providing conservation context for plant identifications. All AI insights are enrichment on top of real data; they degrade gracefully if the service is offline.

Data quality

Gaps from power outages are automatically backfilled on the next script run using archive APIs. The database uses upsert logic — re-running any script never creates duplicates. All data stored with IST (+05:30) timestamps. The combined_readings view merges Weather Union and Open-Meteo seamlessly, with WU taking priority where available. Every large table is paginated — no silent truncation.

Built from scratch, on a balcony

Setup steps

Flash Raspberry Pi OS Lite (64-bit)
Use Raspberry Pi Imager. Enable SSH and Wi-Fi during flash. Boot and connect via SSH.
ssh pi@[your-pi-ip]
Clone the Earth Pulse repository
All scripts, cron jobs, and config files are in the public GitHub repo.
git clone https://github.com/fangchu-in/earth-pulse
Create your .env file
You'll need a Google Air Quality API key and your NODE_ID (assigned by us). The README walks through each service.
cp .env.example .env && nano .env
Run the setup script
Installs Python dependencies, configures cron jobs, tests sensors, verifies database connectivity. Around 10 minutes.
bash setup.sh
Register your node
Once data is flowing, use the form below. We'll assign your NODE_ID, and your data joins the network within 24 hours.
