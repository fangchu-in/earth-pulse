Baner Hill stands at 678 metres — the third highest point within Pune city limits — on the edge of the Western Ghats, one of the world's eight "hottest hotspots" of biodiversity and a UNESCO World Heritage ecosystem older than the Himalayas.

— photos · —

Why this hill matters

The Western Ghats — of which Baner Hill is an outlier spur — harbour 30% of India's plant, fish, herpetofauna, bird, and mammal species on less than 6% of the country's land. These mountains pre-date the Himalayas and represent ancient, unique biophysical and ecological processes that have evolved over hundreds of millions of years.

Despite their ecological significance, the Ghats face rapid urban pressure. Baner Hill, rising just above one of Pune's fastest-growing suburbs, is a front-row seat to that tension.

What Earth Pulse is doing

Every photo in this archive is timestamped and matched with the air quality, temperature, humidity, rainfall, and wind recorded by Earth Pulse sensors at that exact moment. Over time this builds a long-term visual and environmental record of the hill.

Green in monsoon, brown in summer, shrouded in fog at dawn — the hill tells a story that numbers alone cannot. Earth Pulse has so far detected — bird species from this hillside using acoustic AI, contributing to our understanding of biodiversity in a rapidly urbanising landscape.

1990 — The Baseline
A Hill of Rock and Dust
NDVI 0.074. Healthy tropical forest scores above 0.70 — dense Amazon canopy reaches 0.90+. Baner Hill in 1990 scored lower than a dirt road. This is what Landsat-5 measured, band by band, pixel by pixel, from 705 km above the Earth. Not an estimate. A measurement.
1990–2015 — The Long Silence
25 Years of Barely Moving Numbers
NDVI hovered between 0.074 and 0.154 — slow natural colonisation of pioneer grass, repeatedly knocked back. The 2010 crash from 0.154 to 0.090 erased 6 years of fragile recovery in one season. The hill had no resilience. It needed root depth, soil organic matter, and canopy — all of which only come from deliberate, sustained planting.
2017 — The Inflection Point
When Human Work Became Visible from Space
NDVI jumped from 0.138 to 0.211 — a 53% rise in two years. Scrub does not do this spontaneously on semi-arid laterite. This is the satellite recording volunteers carrying water uphill, fencing saplings against grazing, returning every monsoon. Planted trees reached a size where their canopy finally registered on Sentinel-2. After 2017, gains did not reverse. The hill had crossed a threshold of ecological resilience.
2021–2025 — The Acceleration
Compounding Ecological Returns
NDVI jumped from 0.215 to 0.268 between 2019 and 2021 — the largest two-year gain ever recorded here. This is reforestation ecology compounding: canopy creates shade, shade retains soil moisture, moisture allows understory, understory attracts seed-dispersing birds, birds bring new species. Each layer makes the next easier. The 2025 dry-season reading of 0.278 is 3.7× the 1990 baseline — in the season when the hill should look least green.
The monsoon pulse: NDVI 0.957. During August 2025, Sentinel-2 recorded individual pixels on the north slope at 0.957 — within the range of dense Amazon rainforest. The whole-hill mean reached 0.480 during monsoon: a 94% surge above the dry reading. This is what a recovering forest does — it inhales every monsoon with increasing intensity. In 1990 there was nothing to inhale with.
The Target — How Far Remains
0.55 by 2035: The Recovery Goal
A dry-season NDVI of 0.55 represents mature secondary forest — the ecological equivalent of a functioning Ghats forest patch. Baner Hill at 0.278 is 51% of the way there. At the current rate of +0.005 NDVI/year, the natural trajectory reaches 0.55 around 2080. Targeted planting in the south and south-west zones — still at 0.11–0.14 — can cut that timeline significantly. Every sapling in the right zone changes the number.

🌱 Plant Here — Where the Hill Needs You Most

The NDVI heatmap above is not just a scientific image — it is a volunteer deployment map. Every orange and yellow zone is a call to action. These areas have NDVI below 0.25 and have not recovered since 1990. They will not recover without intervention.

South-West Corridor
0.11 NDVI

South-Facing Mid-Slope

Direct solar exposure all day. Loses moisture fastest. Needs drought-tolerant deep-rooters. Recommended: Neem, Pongamia, Bel. Plant in clusters of 5 to create shared shade.
🟡 Priority 3 — Gap-Fill & Support
East Face Trail Corridor
0.38 NDVI
Recovering but patchy. Trail-edge erosion visible. Recommended: Bamboo for slope stabilisation, Hirda, Indian Gooseberry. Check survival of prior plantings.
🟢 Protect — Do Not Disturb
North Slope & Summit
0.67–0.72 NDVI
Dense established canopy. Monsoon peak reaches 0.957 here. This is what the whole hill can become. Protect from encroachment and overgrazing. Focus volunteer energy on south zones.
