Frequently Asked Questions

How many bird species have been detected in Baner, Pune?
Earth Pulse has acoustically detected over 88 bird species in Baner, Pune using Cornell Lab's BirdNET AI. Detections come from two sensor nodes on the Sahyadri foothills, recording continuously day and night since 2025. The count updates automatically as new species are detected.
What birds can be found near Baner Hill, Pune?
Common species detected near Baner Hill include the Asian Koel, Red-vented Bulbul, Indian Robin, Ashy Prinia, Purple Sunbird, Common Myna, Black Drongo, Red-wattled Lapwing, and Greater Coucal. Migratory species like warblers, pipits, and flycatchers pass through seasonally.
When is the best time to hear birds in Baner, Pune?
Over 60% of bird detections in Baner occur during the dawn chorus between 5am and 8am IST. A secondary peak occurs between 5pm and 7pm. The monsoon season (June–September) brings the highest species diversity to Baner Hill.
How does Earth Pulse detect birds?
Earth Pulse uses Raspberry Pi computers running Cornell Lab's BirdNET AI v2.4 to continuously record and analyse audio from the Sahyadri foothills in Baner, Pune. Two microphones face east and south towards Baner Hill. Each detection is logged with confidence score, timestamp, AQI, and temperature.
Are there owls and nocturnal birds on Baner Hill?
Yes — Earth Pulse monitors continuously overnight, capturing owl species, nightjars, and nocturnal migrants on Baner Hill. Select the "Night (9pm–4am)" filter above to see nocturnal species only.
Which birds are most active after rain on Baner Hill?
Earth Pulse cross-references Weather Union rainfall data with acoustic detections. Post-rain conditions at Baner Hill show measurably higher bird detection rates within 2–4 hours, particularly for ground-feeding species responding to increased invertebrate activity after rainfall.
What is the Pulse Score and how does it relate to birds?
The Earth Pulse Score (0–100) combines air quality, bird activity, and clean air hours into a single weekly health metric for Baner. Higher bird detection counts contribute positively to the score, making it a proxy for ecological health. See the weekly score in our weekly blog reports.

About the Bird Detection Data

Earth Pulse is a citizen science environmental observatory in Baner, Pune, Maharashtra, India — on the edge of the Sahyadri range of the Western Ghats. Two Raspberry Pi sensor nodes run Cornell Lab's BirdNET AI continuously, recording audio from balconies facing Baner Hill. Every bird call is analysed, identified to species level, and logged with confidence score, timestamp, AQI, and temperature.

Birds of the Western Ghats — Baner, Pune

Baner sits at the biodiversity transition zone between the Western Ghats biodiversity hotspot and Pune's urban landscape. Species commonly detected include the Asian Koel (Eudynamys scolopaceus), Red-vented Bulbul (Pycnonotus cafer), Indian Robin (Copsychus fulicatus), Ashy Prinia (Prinia socialis), Purple Sunbird (Cinnyris asiaticus), Red-wattled Lapwing (Vanellus indicus), and Common Myna (Acridotheres tristis). Migratory species pass through during winter (October–February) and monsoon (June–September).

Methodology

BirdNET v2.4 by the Cornell Lab of Ornithology identifies bird species from 5-second audio windows. Data is stored in a Supabase PostgreSQL database and updated in real time. Air quality data is sourced from Google Air Quality API and Open-Meteo CAMS. Weather data from Weather Union station ZWL008983.
