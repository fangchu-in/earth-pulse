Why migratory birds matter

🌍
Living climate sensors
Migrants respond to temperature and food availability across two continents. Shifts in their arrival dates at Baner are direct evidence of climate change — data that matters globally.
🌱
Seed dispersers & pollinators
Even in winter, migrants disperse seeds and control insect populations. A single flycatcher can consume thousands of insects during its 4-month stay on Baner Hill.
🔗
Ecosystem connectors
Migratory birds physically link Baner's ecosystem to forests in Kazakhstan, Siberia, and the Himalayas. Their health reflects the state of habitats across the entire flyway.
📊
Conservation indicators
Declining migrant numbers at a site signal habitat degradation long before it becomes visible. Earth Pulse detection data year-over-year provides an early warning system for Baner Hill.

Threats to migrants at Baner

💡
Light pollutionArtificial lighting on the hill face disorients night-migrating birds, causing them to circle exhausted around lit buildings and miss their stopover. October–November is the peak risk window.
🏗️
Scrub clearanceDense native scrub — Ziziphus, Carissa, Lantana — is the primary foraging habitat for winter warblers and flycatchers. Clearing it during September–November removes their food and shelter on arrival.
🐱
Feral cat predationExhausted migrants that land after a long night flight are highly vulnerable to cat predation. The post-arrival period (October–November) is when risk is highest.
🚜
Habitat fragmentationBaner Hill is increasingly isolated from adjacent green areas by construction. As the corridor narrows, migrants have fewer options to land, refuel, and continue their journey.
🌡️
Climate disruptionWarmer October temperatures delay insect emergence — the food that migrants depend on after arrival. Recent years have seen late arrivals and erratic winter occurrence at Pune's tekdis, consistent with national trends.

Baner Hill sits near the southern end of the Central Asian Flyway — one of the world's busiest bird migration corridors, stretching from Arctic Siberia to peninsular India. These birds travel 3,000–8,000 km to spend winter here.

Making Baner Hill a migratory haven

Every action taken to restore native vegetation and reduce disturbance on Baner Hill directly benefits birds that have flown 5,000 km to reach here. This is a practical guide to what works — grounded in ecology, not sentiment.

🌿 Habitat: what migrants need

🌳
Dense native scrub — do not clear it
Winter warblers (Common Chiffchaff, Blyth's Reed Warbler) forage almost entirely in dense scrub — gleaning caterpillars and small arthropods from leaves and twigs. Native Ziziphus, Carissa, and even Lantana (despite being invasive) provide this habitat. Clearing it in October–November removes their primary foraging ground on arrival after a 5,000 km journey.
🌾
Open grassland patches for pipits & wagtails
Wagtails and pipits feed on open ground — insects in short grass and bare soil. Maintaining some open grassland patches on the hill slopes (not converting everything to dense planting) is essential for ground-foraging migrants.
🌳
Plant native insect-rich trees
Neem (Azadirachta indica), Indian Gooseberry (Phyllanthus emblica), and Jamun (Syzygium cumini) host rich communities of bark insects, scale insects, and caterpillars — exactly what flycatchers and warblers feed on. See our trees guide →
💧
Water — critical for exhausted migrants
Birds that have flown through the night need to drink immediately on landing. A birdbath with clean, fresh water in your garden or building terrace can be a lifesaver for arriving migrants in October–November. Keep it clean and filled daily throughout winter.

💡 Lighting: reduce it during migration

🌙
Turn off unnecessary lights 9pm–5am in Oct–Nov
Most small migrants — warblers, flycatchers, pipits — travel at night using star patterns and magnetic fields for navigation. Artificial light disrupts both systems, causing birds to circle exhausted around lit buildings, collide with glass, or miss their stopover entirely. The most impactful window is October 15 – November 30.
🏢
High-rise buildings are the greatest collision risk
Tall glass buildings with interior lighting are the primary cause of migrant mortality in urban areas. Closing curtains or blinds on upper floors at night significantly reduces collision risk. This is already practised during migration season in cities like New York (Lights Out program) and Toronto.
🔦
Switch to warm-tone LED lighting
Cool-white and blue-spectrum LEDs disorient migrants more than warm amber lighting. If lighting is required on the hill face or in gardens, switching to warm amber (2700K) LEDs reduces the impact on migrating birds while maintaining visibility.

📢 Community action

📊
Record what you see — submit to eBird
Every bird sighting submitted to eBird.org or the eBird India app contributes to State of India's Birds assessments that influence national conservation policy. Earth Pulse acoustic data feeds into this picture — your visual observations complete it.
🌳
Plant one native tree this monsoon
The best time to plant for migrants is the monsoon (July–September), so trees are established before the first arrivals in October. Focus on insect-rich natives: Neem, Amla, Jamun, Peepal, Ficus. Contact Pugmarks Society or local forest department for Baner Hill native saplings.
🗣️
Advocate for construction moratoriums in Oct–Nov
Construction noise and vibration on the hill slopes during peak arrival (October–November) displaces migrants before they can refuel. Raising this with the Pune Municipal Corporation — backed by Earth Pulse data on migrant arrivals — is a legitimate, evidence-based conservation ask.
🐱
Keep cats indoors during Oct–Nov
Exhausted migrants are easy prey. A domestic cat allowed outside during October–November can kill dozens of birds in a single migration season — birds that have survived a 5,000 km journey. Keeping cats indoors during this window is the single easiest individual action.

Migratory Birds of Baner Hill, Pune — Ecological Context

Baner Hill in Pune, Maharashtra sits near the southern end of the Central Asian Flyway — one of the world's major bird migration corridors connecting Arctic Siberia to peninsular India. The hill is a designated urban forest and sits at the ecotone between the Western Ghats biodiversity hotspot and the Deccan plateau, making it an ecologically significant stopover for winter migrants travelling south from Central Asia, Russia, and the Himalayas.

Key Migratory Species at Baner Hill

Regularly recorded migratory species at Baner Hill and adjacent Pune tekdis include: Common Chiffchaff (Phylloscopus collybita) arriving from Northern Europe and Western Siberia; Blyth's Reed Warbler (Acrocephalus dumetorum) from Central Russia and Kazakhstan; Asian Brown Flycatcher (Muscicapa dauurica) from Mongolia and East Asia; Red-breasted Flycatcher (Ficedula parva) from Eastern Europe; Verditer Flycatcher (Eumyias thalassinus) from the Himalayas; and various pipit and wagtail species from the Himalayan foothills. Migratory raptors including Common Kestrel, Eurasian Hobby, and Booted Eagle have been recorded at nearby Vetal Tekdi and Mahatma Hill in Pune.

Conservation Status

Migratory bird populations globally are under pressure. Of the species assessed for the State of India's Birds 2020 report, a significant proportion show long-term population decline. Urban green spaces like Baner Hill play an increasingly important role as stopover and wintering sites as natural habitats along flyways are degraded. Earth Pulse acoustic monitoring provides year-over-year phenology data on migrant arrival and departure dates — a valuable long-term dataset for understanding climate-driven changes in migration timing at this location.
